<h2 class="f-ha">
<a href="/tto/business/economics/article3668200.ece">
Recovery into reverse as economy dips by 0.3 per cent
</a>
</h2>
<div class="byline">
<strong class="f-author" ><a href="http://www.thetimes.co.uk/tto/public/profile/Kathryn-Hopkins" class="fg-">Kathryn Hopkins</a></strong>
<span class="title" >Economics Correspondent </span>
<div class="f-regular-update">
Updated 21 minutes ago
</div>
</div>
<div class="ellipsis"><p>The economy shrank by more than expected in the final three months of last 
year, raising fears that Britain is on the verge of entering an 
unprecedented triple-dip recession. 
Gross Domestic Product (GDP) contracted by 0.3 per cent between October and 
December after a 0.9 per cent rise in the previous quarter, boosted by 
one-off factors such as the Olympic Games. 
Analysts had been expecting a quarterly drop of 0.1 per cent, but the 
worse-than-expected figures now mean that for 2012 as a whole the economy 
was flat. 
If GDP shrinks again between January and March, it would mark the third 
technical recession, defined as two consecutive quarters of negative growth, 
in only five years and would be the first time Britain has suffered a triple 
dip since records began in 1955. 
A Treasury spokesperson said: “It confirms what we already knew - that 
Britain, like many European countries, still faces a very difficult economic 
situation. It underlines what the Chancellor said at the Autumn Statement 
and the Governor of the Bank of England said this week: while the economy is 
healing, it is a difficult road.” 
The figures raise fears that Britain could lose its prized triple-A credit 
rating. Last month Standard &amp; Poor’s became the last of the big three 
credit rating agencies to put the UK’s rating on “negative” watch, citing 
concerns over the country’s weak recovery and the state of the public 
finances. 
Chris Williamson,</p></div>
